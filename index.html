2020 NFL Penalty Analysis

The NFL is a highly competitive league and extremely exciting as a result. There are so many aspects of the game that provide intrigue from the games, players, front offices, refs, and fans. The league can manufacture drama into almost anything (as I write this they are literally about to televise a schedule release), so there's no wonder that it is the most popular sport in the U.S. There is also a lot of analytics involved with the NFL, from internal team analytics that are used when making decisions to public analytics that are used for journalism and things like fantasy football. There are a lot of different aspects that you can focus on in football and there's usually a lot of information to be found concerning those areas. Except, I've always felt that no one really talks about penalties that much. Sure, fans complain about refs incessantly and blame them for all their and their team's problems, but I've never really seen penatlies examined in an in depth way. That's what this tutorial is going to be about (I can't promise it will be deep but I'll try my best), looking at penalties and asking pertinent questions about them. I must note that because I am a Ravens fan, a lot of the questions I ask will be related to/influenced by them.

Data Collection:¶

The data I am going to use for this tutorial is going to come from nflfastpy, a Python package for loading NFL play by play data, schedule data, roster data, and team logo data from nflfastR. nflfastR is itself a package for scraping NFL data, but because it's made for R, I need to use nflfastpy as a wrapper to access the data. Thankfully, loading the pbp (play by play) data into a data frame is pretty simple and does not require any cleaning or tidying. There's a wealth of information available in the pbp data, but I'll only be focusing on a few values that are most pertinent to our analysis.

There doesn't really appear to be a correlation between net penalty yards and win percentage, at least not a linear one. So whether you're being helped by penalties, or hurt by them, or neither, it doesn't realy affect your chances of winning. However there is a problem with this analysis, and just using raw penalty yards in general.

The problem with raw penalty yards¶

When you're watching a game, you'll often see a graphic like this:

Now, while this can definitely be informative (and this graphic in particular was an apt depiction of the impact penalties had in the game), that isn't always the case. In fact, my motivation for looking at penalties was exactly because of graphics like this. I would be watching a game and see that the two teams had a similar amount of penalties and/or penalty yards, and think to myself, "But that really doesn't do a good job of capturing the impact of those penalties."

To illustrate this let me pose a question: Would you rather have a 5 yard definsive holding penalty against you or a 50 yard defensive pass interference against you? Both result in an automatic first down for the opposing offense, it's just that one is 45 yards further down the field than the other. The intuitive answer seems to be that you would take the 5 yard penalty, and in some (and maybe most) instances you would be correct. But the idea that I'm trying to get at is that the impact of a penalty is largely dependent on the circumstances wherein which it occurs. Let's say that 50 yard DPI was early in the first quarter of a game; it would still be bad but not something you couldn't recover from. Let's also say that the 5 yard defensive holding occured in the same game on a 4th and goal with 1 minute to go in the game; this would likely lose you the game. Yet, if you just looked at the raw penalty yards you would think that the DPI was 10x worse than the holding penalty. So what I want to do is find a better way to evaluate the impact that penalties have on games.
